## Do You Actually Need a Monitor Arm?

If you care about desk space and neck comfort — yes.

The stock stand that ships with most monitors is functional but wasteful. It occupies a fixed footprint on the desk surface, positions the monitor at a fixed height (usually too low for comfortable sitting eye level), and contributes to the cable clutter behind the desk. A monitor arm removes the stand entirely, floats the monitor at any height you set, and opens the area under the monitor for use.

The ergonomic case is straightforward: looking down at a monitor positioned too low causes neck and upper back strain over long study sessions. The correct monitor position is eye level to the top third of the screen. Most stock stands can't achieve this without a riser or stack of books — a monitor arm does it natively and adjustably.

The desk space benefit is most noticeable in a dorm room. A typical monitor stand base is 8 to 10 inches deep. Removing it and replacing it with an arm clamp creates a usable strip of desk that didn't exist before. In a 60-inch desk shared with a laptop, a textbook, a lamp, and a notebook, that strip matters.

## What Weight Monitor Can Each Arm Hold?

All three arms on this list hold up to 17 to 17.6 lbs — sufficient for virtually every 24 to 27-inch monitor in the under-$200 category, which typically weighs 8 to 12 lbs without the stand.

To check your monitor's weight: find the model number on the back of the panel and look up the specs page — the weight without stand is the relevant number. Most student monitors fall well within the 17 lb limit of any arm on this list. Monitors over 27 inches or ultrawide panels may exceed these limits — check before buying.

The Ergotron LX ships in variants rated for different weight ranges. The standard LX handles up to 7.7 kg; a heavy-duty version handles up to 11.3 kg (25 lbs) for larger displays. If you have a large monitor, verify the specific Ergotron variant you're ordering.

## How to Set Up a Monitor at the Correct Eye Level

The target: top of the monitor at or slightly below eye level when sitting in your normal working posture.

**Step 1:** Sit in your chair at your desk in the position you actually work in — not the ideal upright posture, your actual posture.

**Step 2:** Look straight ahead. That's the target zone for the center of your screen. The top of the monitor should be at or just below eye level so your gaze falls naturally at the upper third of the screen.

**Step 3:** Adjust the arm height until the monitor matches. Ergotron LX: loosen the tension slightly, lift the arm to height, re-lock. Budget arms: loosen the height adjustment bolt, set position, retighten.

**Step 4:** Set tilt so the screen faces your eyes directly — a slight upward tilt of 10 to 20 degrees is typical for monitors positioned at or just below eye level.

**Step 5:** Set depth. The monitor should be roughly an arm's length away — 20 to 30 inches from your eyes is the standard recommendation. Pull it closer for detail work, push it back to reduce eye strain during long sessions.

## Monitor Arm vs Monitor Stand — Which Is Better?

A monitor arm is better in almost every situation where you have a desk that supports one.

**Monitor stand advantages:** Zero installation, no desk clamping, no VESA plate removal. Useful for desks with drawers that block clamps, glass desks, or situations where you move the monitor frequently between locations.

**Monitor arm advantages:** Adjustable height (stands are fixed or limited), full repositioning in seconds, reclaimed desk surface under the monitor, integrated or clip-based cable management, and portrait/landscape rotation. For a permanent desk setup, the arm wins on every dimension that matters.

The one scenario where a stand is clearly better: you move your monitor regularly — between home and campus, or between rooms. Mounting and dismounting an arm repeatedly is annoying. A stand stays put and travels easily.
